### STEP 1 — Choosing Fabric: The Best Quilted Textiles for a Hooded Jacket

For this hooded jacket design, pre-quilted fabrics are ideal — offering warmth, structure, and texture without the need for extra padding. Each fabric type adds its own character, letting you adapt the jacket to your personal style and the season.

**Pre-Quilted Cotton** is soft, breathable, and easy to sew — ideal for transitional weather and everyday wear. This fabric creates a light quilted hooded jacket that can be layered comfortably with a cotton or voile lining. **Pre-Quilted Denim** adds a modern, structured look with casual durability and a stylish textured surface, pairing beautifully with a smooth satin or lightweight polyester lining.

For something more versatile, **Pre-Quilted Nylon or Polyester** is lightweight and weather-resistant — perfect for a sporty or travel-friendly hooded jacket. And for cooler months, **Pre-Quilted Wool or Wool Blends** offer natural warmth, refined drape, and a luxurious finish that pairs beautifully with a silky or quilted lining for extra comfort and insulation.

### STEP 4 — How to Sew Patch Pockets with Bias Binding

Great jacket patterns feature functional, stylish pockets — and for this quilted sewing project, we elevate the standard patch pocket by finishing all edges with bias binding. Begin by measuring the pocket top and sides and cutting two pieces of bias band to match those lengths.

Open one folded edge of the bias band, align it with the raw edge of the top pocket with right sides together, and stitch along the crease. Fold the band over to completely encase the raw fabric and topstitch close to the folded edge. Repeat the process for the sides and bottom edge of the pocket, folding the ends 1 cm inward before topstitching for a crisp, professional corner finish.

Once your pocket edges are fully bound, transfer the pocket placement markings from the pattern to the jacket front. Place the pocket wrong side to right side on the designated spot and topstitch it neatly into place, stitching 0.1 cm away from the edge for a clean, barely-there seam line.

### STEP 5 — How to Sew Set-In Sleeves on a Quilted Jacket

With the pockets secured and the shoulder seams joined, we move on to the sleeves. Lay the back piece flat and place the front piece on top, right sides facing. Carefully align the shoulder seams and pin them in place, then stitch with a 1 cm seam allowance. Press the seam open with an iron for a smooth, flat result — and repeat the same process for your lining pieces.

To set in the sleeves, position the sleeve cap into the armhole with the wrong sides facing each other. Align the notches on both the sleeve and the armhole carefully, then secure the sleeve in position from both sides using pins. Sew along the edges, maintaining a consistent 0.7 cm seam allowance throughout for a perfectly balanced, professional silhouette.

### STEP 6 — How to Sew and Attach a Softly Shaped Hood

The hood gives this garment its signature look and is one of the most satisfying parts of the entire construction. Begin with the outer hood pieces: place them right sides together and sew along the curved center seam using a 1 cm seam allowance. Repeat the exact same process for the lining hood pieces.

Once both the outer and lining hoods are stitched, open them up and place them right sides together, carefully matching the center seams. Sew along the front face edge with a 1 cm seam allowance, then turn the hood right side out and press the edge flat for a clean, polished line. Serge the bottom raw edge of the hood to prepare it for attachment to the jacket.

To attach the hood to the coat body, fold the neckline in half to mark the center point, then do the same for the hood. With right sides together, match both center points and pin the hood evenly along the neckline. Sew in place using a 0.5 cm seam allowance for a smooth, seamless attachment.

### STEP 7 — How to Fully Line a Jacket (The Sandwich Method)

A fully lined interior is what separates a truly polished fashion pattern from a basic sewing project — it elevates your quilted sewing to a professional level. To achieve this without any raw seams showing on the inside, we use a clean bagging-out technique.

Place your assembled main jacket flat on a surface with the hood positioned downward and the sleeves folded inward. Take the fully assembled lining piece and place it directly on top, right sides together, aligning the center front and neckline edges carefully and securing them with pins. Make sure the lining sleeves are also tucked inside, creating a sandwich-like arrangement where everything is fully enclosed.

Begin sewing from the bottom hem on the left side and continue around the back, ensuring all edges are evenly matched. Trim the seam allowance along the curved edges for a smooth finish, then pull the entire jacket right-side out through the bottom hem opening and press all edges with an iron for crisp, clean lines.

### STEP 8 — Finishing Hems and Openings with Bias Binding

For the absolute highest quality finish on your quilted sewing project, we apply bias binding to both the wrist hems and the entire front opening of the jacket — from the hem, up along both front edges, and around the hood face opening. This is the detail that gives the LU2COHOUSE Hooded Quilted Jacket its refined, handcrafted character.

Begin with the wrist hems: align the wrist edges of the lining and main fabric, right sides together, and secure with pins. Cut a bias band to match the wrist hem length, open one folded edge, align its raw edge with the raw edge of the wrist hem (right sides together), pin neatly, and stitch along the crease line. Fold the bias band to the inside, covering the raw edge and previous stitch line, then topstitch close to the folded edge to secure both layers firmly. Press for a neat, polished finish.

For the front opening and hood, measure the full continuous length — front opening, hood, and hemline — and cut a single bias band to match. Open one folded edge, align it with the raw edges of the jacket, stitch, and fold it over to the inside to completely encase all raw seams. Topstitch it down for a durable, beautifully handcrafted touch that ensures your jacket lasts for years.
